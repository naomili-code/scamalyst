About Scamalyst

Where This Started

For the past two years, I have served as a Tech Tutor through a local town library program, working one-on-one with community members to navigate modern technology and stay safe online.

Many people I support‚Äîespecially older adults and immigrants‚Äîfeel overwhelmed by how quickly digital tools change and worry about scams. I help with practical tasks like using phones, recognizing suspicious emails, navigating websites, and interacting safely online.

The Pattern I Kept Seeing

Nearly every person I supported struggled to identify what was safe online and what was fraudulent.

People regularly showed scam emails impersonating banks, fake pop-up virus warnings, and urgent messages designed to trigger fear.

Why It Became Personal

My interest in coding and cybersecurity started with curiosity about how systems are built‚Äîand how attackers exploit them.

Working directly with real people turned that curiosity into responsibility: I wanted to build tools that protect, not just tools that impress.

Why I Built Scamalyst

Scamalyst is designed to explain risks, not just label them. The goal is to help users build long-term confidence, not short-term panic.

It was built so people can understand red flags in plain language and make safer choices in daily digital life.

Who This Is For

Older Adults

Simple, jargon-free guidance for everyday online safety.

Immigrants & Non-Native Speakers

Clear explanations of language tricks scammers use.

Students

Hands-on practice with realistic examples and feedback.

Everyday Users

No sign-ups, no downloads, and no complicated setup.

What This Experience Changed

This role shaped my goals by showing me that technology is most meaningful when it serves others.

I am driven by one idea: everyone deserves to feel confident and safe in the digital world.
